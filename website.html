MOTIVATION

Motor vehicle crashes are a leading cause of death. To address this, we aim to analyze trends in traffic accidents, focusing on:

  • Seasonal patterns
  • Time-of-day and day-of-week trends
  • Effects of weather, policy changes, etc.

By examining these trends, we hope to identify potential risks and inform policymaking to reduce injuries and fatalities.

General Trend

There's a clear seasonal pattern over the last 7 years, with more crashes during the summer months than the winter months. The only notable abnormality is 2020 due to Covid.

(October 2025 has a low crash count because the dataset only included data up until October 24, 2025)

Average Hourly Crashes

Weekdays (Mon-Fri) show a consistent pattern, with most crashes occurring during the morning and afternoon rush hours, around 8 a.m. and 4 p.m. This pattern disappears on weekends.

Overall, these trends are expected, as traffic volumes are much higher during daytime than at night.

Crashes Conditioned on Weather

Performing a paired t-test between Clear and Rainy hourly crashes resulted in a t-statistic of -12.64 and a p-value of 7.8 × 10-12. Similarly, a paired t-test between Clear and Snowy hourly crashes resulted in a t-statistic of -3.65 and a p-value of 1.3 × 10-3. A paired t-test between Rainy and Snowy hours did not show any significant difference.

(Note: This graph assumes similar amount of traffic on clear and rainy/snowy days, which may underestimate the effect of rain/snow on crashes.)

Linear Regression

Both rain and snow are slightly positively correlated with car crashes.

Daily Crash Count

While all three weather conditions have similar medians, the distributions differ. Specifically, rainy and snowy days appear to have a higher minimum number of crashes.
